## Executive Summary

MAHAMAIA Systems builds **LeWM-VC**, a machine-native video codec for surveillance infrastructure. It compresses video frames into a compact semantic latent representation — preserving what machine perception systems need (object location, class, trajectory) while discarding what they don't (fine texture, color fidelity). ## The Problem

### Video Is Transitioning from a Human Medium to a Machine Medium

Global video traffic now exceeds 82% of internet data. An accelerating fraction of this video — generated by surveillance cameras, autonomous vehicles, industrial sensors, and smart-city platforms — is never viewed by a human being. It is processed exclusively by machine perception systems: object detectors, trackers, anomaly classifiers, and behavior analytics engines running on GPU clusters.

This transition is not a trend. It is structural. A 100-camera surveillance deployment generates more footage in a day than a human team can review in a year. Machine perception is the only operationally viable model at scale. The question is not whether AI will consume this video. It already does. The question is whether the compression infrastructure will be designed for the actual consumer.

### The Codec Stack Was Built for the Wrong Consumer

H.264, H.265, and H.266 are extraordinary engineering achievements — the product of decades of optimization for human visual perception. They minimize pixel-level distortion as measured by PSNR, preserve high-frequency texture and fine spatial detail that human eyes notice.

None of this is what a GPU object detector needs.

A detector cares about object location, aspect ratio, spatial configuration, and class-discriminative features — low-frequency semantic structure that codecs routinely discard in favor of high-frequency texture that humans find pleasing. The consequence is double waste: bits spent preserving information the machine cannot use, and bits not spent on semantic structure the machine needs.

This mismatch is quantifiable. At 1.95 bits per pixel — a common operating point for surveillance archive storage — a probe trained on H.265-decoded frames achieves 79.3% object class accuracy. The same probe on LeWM-VC at matched bitrate achieves 86.5%. The codec built for humans loses 7.2 percentage points of machine perception quality to pixels that humans never see.

### The Standards Gap

MPEG Video Coding for Machines (VCM) is the standards community's formal recognition of this problem. Once VCM is ratified, every camera OEM, VMS vendor, and smart city platform will face a certification requirement. Simultaneously, the EU AI Act — now entering enforcement — requires technical documentation of task accuracy for high-risk AI systems, including surveillance. There is no standardized way to produce this documentation today. The gap is a market.

## The Solution

### LeWM-VC: A Codec Built for Machine Perception

LeWM-VC is a video codec that treats the compressed bitstream as a machine-native feature stream rather than a visual approximation. Each video frame is represented as a compact latent grid of shape 192×16×16, produced by a Vision Transformer encoder with 16×16 patch resolution. Temporal redundancy is exploited through a learned transformer predictor that forecasts the next frame's latent from up to four previous decoded latents — no hand-crafted motion vectors, no optical flow. Only the prediction residual is transmitted, using a 2-component Gaussian Mixture Model entropy coder.

The practical results on PEViD-HD surveillance video at 256×256 resolution:

| Metric                                          | Value   | Context                               |
| ----------------------------------------------- | ------- | ------------------------------------- |
| P-frame bitrate reduction (vs. all-intra)       | 62%     | GOP=8, matched reconstruction quality |
| P/I frame bit ratio                             | 0.37×   | Predictor pre-training essential      |
| Class accuracy advantage over H.265 at 1.95 BPP | +7.2 pp | 86.5% vs. 79.3%, matched bitrate      |
| Class accuracy advantage over H.265 at 0.11 BPP | +1.7 pp | 94.4% vs. 92.7%, operational bitrate  |
| Inference throughput (NVIDIA T4)                | 80+ fps | Real-time capable on edge hardware    |
| Total parameters                                | 14.7M   | Edge-deployable                       |
| Peak GPU memory (inference)                     | 1.2 GB  | Fits standard edge accelerators       |
| Memory bandwidth reduction                      | >99%    | 12 KB/frame vs. 1.5 MB/frame raw      |

A 500-camera deployment at $8/camera/month saves approximately $120K/year in storage costs while improving detection accuracy. At $5–15/camera/month, the payback period is measured in months against storage and compute savings alone.

All results are reproducible from public checkpoints. Exact command lines, random seeds, and checkpoint hashes are documented in the repository.

**Honest scope of current results:** The evaluation above is based on training across two PEViD-HD surveillance video clips — a proof of concept demonstrating architectural validity, not a production benchmark. Dataset expansion to the full PEViD-HD corpus and VIRAT Ground 2.0 is underway (expected completion Q3 2026).

### LeWM-Eval: The Evaluation Framework That Ships with the Codec

LeWM-Eval is a reproducible evaluation framework that measures task accuracy at matched bitrate rather than pixel fidelity. It is codec-agnostic: any codec — traditional or learned — can be evaluated through a standard interface. This means our customers can verify our numbers independently and any competitor can be benchmarked against LeWM-VC using the same methodology. We publish those comparisons.

Its core innovation is methodological: training identical lightweight CNN probes against frozen teacher detector pseudo-labels across both the codec under evaluation and the baseline, at matched BPP. This produces a fair, reproducible comparison of information content rather than optimization quality.

Current components: semantic probing (objectness and class accuracy), bitrate matching within ±5% BPP, cross-teacher validation.

LeWM-Eval is not a separate product. It is the measurement tool that comes with the codec.

## Technology and Moat

### Why This Architecture, Why Now

Three enabling conditions converged in 2024–2026 that make LeWM-VC viable in a way it was not previously:

**JEPA maturation.** Joint Embedding Predictive Architectures (LeCun 2022; LeWM 2025) demonstrated that predicting future states in compact latent space — without reconstructing pixels — produces stable, semantically rich representations. LeWM-VC extends this to video compression.

**Edge compute cost crossover.** NVIDIA Jetson Orin and AMD Ryzen AI NPU make real-time transformer inference viable at the camera level. A 14.7M parameter model at 80+ fps is deployable on current edge hardware at commodity prices. This was not true in 2022.

**MPEG VCM formalization.** The standardization process creates a compliance market that did not exist before 2023. VCM creates demand for machine-oriented evaluation, which accelerates adoption of the framework that ships with the codec.

### Technical Defensibility

The moat has three layers, ordered by when they become effective:

**Layer 1 — Domain-specific deployment data.** A codec trained on real surveillance data learns surveillance-specific temporal dynamics (pedestrian motion patterns, fixed-camera parallax, lighting cycles) that a general-purpose codec cannot replicate without equivalent data. This creates a data flywheel: more deployment data improves the model, which improves compression, which drives more deployment. This is the near-term moat, and it starts with the first design partner.

**Layer 2 — Deployment data.** A codec trained on real surveillance data learns dynamics that competitors cannot replicate without equivalent deployment. This is the near-term moat.

**Layer 3 — IP.** A provisional patent on the JEPA temporal prediction architecture applied to video compression is being filed. This is a timing moat, not a fundamental moat — it creates priority date and a negotiating position with hardware partners.

### What Ablation Studies Reveal

The ablation results tell a story about which components matter and why. Replacing the GMM entropy model with a simpler Laplace prior increases bitrate 5.6×. Training the temporal predictor jointly from random initialization reduces temporal savings from 62% to 4.4%. Removing affine normalization degrades reconstruction quality by ~1.5 dB. These are not incremental choices — they are evidence of principled architectural design.

## Competition

### Codec Competition

**Traditional codecs (H.265, H.266, AV1):** The problem MAHAMAIA solves, not competitors. H.265 will not add machine-perception features. VCM formalization and AI Act enforcement are the forcing functions that overcome market inertia.

**Learned pixel-reconstruction codecs (DCVC-FM, DVC, CompressAI):** These retain pixel reconstruction as the primary training objective and retain explicit motion compensation. They are building better H.265s, not what MAHAMAIA is building. The risk: a DCVC-adjacent group adds semantic probing to their paper. Mitigation: the evaluation framework is independent of codec architecture — they can add our methodology and it will show their codec does not preserve semantics as well as ours.

**The most dangerous competitive scenario:** A large corporate entity (Qualcomm, Huawei) contributes an evaluation framework to MPEG VCM before LeWM-Eval is established. Mitigation: the defense is customer relationships and deployment data, which create switching costs. The LeWM-Eval moat emerges over time as a secondary defense.

### Benchmark Competition

No external, multi-codec, reproducible evaluation framework for machine-oriented compression exists today. This gap is the opportunity. Expect competition within 12–18 months. The defense is the deployed customer base that already uses LeWM-Eval to verify performance.
