# Conference Review: LeWM-VC — Learned Video Compression via JEPA

> **Date:** 2026-05-16  
> **Context:** Post-arXiv roadmap document. This review was generated by an automated technical reviewer simulating a program-committee-level assessment. It identifies gaps to address before submitting to a top venue (CVPR/ICCV/NeurIPS).

---

## 1. Overall Summary

LeWM-VC proposes a surveillance-domain video codec that replaces traditional block-based motion estimation with temporal prediction in a JEPA latent space, representing each frame as a 192×16×16 compact grid produced by a ViT-Tiny encoder. The principal claims are: (1) 62% bitrate reduction for P-frames over all-intra coding without explicit motion vectors; (2) superior semantic preservation of compressed latents versus x265 at matched bitrate, measured via lightweight CNN probing; and (3) a calibrated surprise-gated quantization mechanism as a training-free adaptive bitrate tool. The work is accompanied by LeWM-Eval, an open-source reproducibility framework.

---

## 2. Key Strengths

**S1 — Clean architectural thesis with good internal consistency (Sections 3.1–3.4).** The design rationale is coherent: eliminate hand-crafted motion vectors by learning temporal dynamics implicitly through a transformer predictor in latent space. The connection to the information bottleneck (Eq. 1) and predictive coding (Section 2.4) provides genuine theoretical grounding, not just post-hoc motivation. The two-phase training protocol (Section 3.4.1) is practically well-motivated and the quantitative justification — prediction MSE dropping from 0.226 to 1.15×10⁻⁴ — makes the necessity of pre-training concrete rather than hand-wavy.

**S2 — Honest, self-aware treatment of limitations (Section 5.5).** The paper explicitly acknowledges domain specificity, the absence of learned codec baselines, UVG generalization failure, threats to validity, bitstream overhead underestimates, and the divergence from the full SIGReg procedure. This level of candor is rare in preprints and substantially raises credibility. The failure mode taxonomy (extreme low-light, rapid pan, dense occlusion) in Section 5.5 is a useful practical contribution.

**S3 — Ablation studies are well-structured and informative (Table 8, Section 4.6).** The four ablations systematically isolate the contributions of GMM vs. Laplace (5.6× BPP impact), predictor pre-training (P/I ratio collapses from 0.37× to 0.93× without it), affine normalization (~1.5 dB), and context length. Each ablation clearly changes one variable and the effects are dramatic enough to be unambiguous. This is better ablation practice than many CVPR/NeurIPS papers.

**S4 — Semantic probing methodology is principled and comparatively fair (Sections 3.7, 4.4).** Training identical lightweight probe architectures against frozen teacher pseudo-labels on both LeWM-VC latents and x265-decoded frames — at matched BPP — is a methodologically sound approach to measuring task-relevant information content. The cross-teacher validation (YOLOv5s vs. YOLOv5su, Tables 6–7) strengthens the claim that the observed advantage is not teacher-specific.

**S5 — Computational profile is practically compelling (Table 9, Section 4.7).** 80+ fps on a T4 GPU with 14.7M parameters, ~12 KB/frame latent footprint, and TensorRT/ONNX compatibility make the deployment case concrete. The memory bandwidth reduction argument (>99% versus raw frames) is a genuine edge deployment advantage that traditional codecs cannot match at equivalent semantic quality.

## 3. Key Weaknesses / Limitations

**W1 — Critically narrow dataset basis (Major).** The entire training corpus is two PEViD-HD video clips (~1,000 frames). This is not a dataset; it is a case study. Any performance numbers reported must be interpreted as proof-of-concept feasibility, not as evidence of generalization within even the surveillance domain. The 100-frame held-out test set is insufficient to compute statistically stable percentile estimates (Section 4.5 reports 99th percentiles from what amounts to a single video's frames). The paper acknowledges this but does not sufficiently foreground it in the abstract or introduction — a reader could easily misread Table 1's headline numbers as generalizable performance claims.

**W2 — Absence of learned codec baselines is a significant gap (Major).** The authors justify comparing only against x265 by calling it "the dominant surveillance deployment target." This is defensible for deployment context but not for research contribution. The paper's contribution is architectural — JEPA-based latent prediction — yet it does not demonstrate whether this architecture outperforms other learned codecs (DVC, DCVC-DC, FVC, or even the CompressAI factorized baseline mentioned only briefly in Section 5.5) on the semantic probing task. A lightweight probe comparison against a similarly-trained DVC or DCVC-DC baseline would substantially strengthen the claim that eliminating motion vectors specifically, rather than neural encoding generally, improves semantic preservation.

**W3 — Surprise metric fails its stated purpose (Major/Moderate).** Section 4.5 reports that mean surprise is 0.596 with a 99th percentile of 0.612, and — critically — that neither the "normal" nor the "anomaly" (bag-drop) video triggers the gating thresholds (τ_low = 0.4, τ_high = 0.8). The surprise-gated quantization mechanism, presented as a key contribution in the abstract and Table 2, therefore never activates on the evaluation data. The paper appropriately notes this requires domain-specific calibration, but it means the mechanism is presented as functional without any evidence of it producing the claimed "∼2× more bits" effect on real data. This is a contribution that does not yet exist empirically.

**W4 — PSNR comparison framing is misleading (Moderate).** The paper repeatedly disclaims PSNR as "not our design target," yet uses it as the primary reconstruction quality metric throughout (Tables 4, 5, 8, 10). At 0.087 BPP, LeWM-VC achieves 25.40 dB — a quality level that is visually poor by any standard and well below what x265 achieves at comparable rates (Table 4 implies x265 reaches 24–25 dB at 0.01–0.021 BPP). The claim that "reconstruction quality improves slightly (25.40 vs. 25.13 dB)" in the abstract characterizes a 0.27 dB difference between the codec's own all-intra and temporal modes — not a comparison to x265 — which a casual reader could misinterpret.

**W5 — Information-theoretic framing (Eq. 1) is disconnected from training (Moderate).** The paper introduces the IB-inspired objective min I(Z;X) s.t. I(Z;Y) ≥ C as an "analytical interpretation" (Section 1) but immediately clarifies that the actual training loss is pixel MSE rate-distortion (Eq. 4). This disconnect is flagged only in a subordinate clause. For a paper claiming to operationalize "the third paradigm" of video coding, the gap between the stated theoretical objective and the implemented one is substantive and should be front-loaded rather than deferred.

## 4. Novelty & Significance

The core novelty — applying JEPA temporal prediction as the motion model in a video codec and demonstrating that compressed latents preserve more semantic information than pixel-reconstructed frames at matched BPP — is genuine and timely. JEPA has been explored for video prediction (V-JEPA, MC-JEPA, C-JEPA) and representation learning, but its application as the temporal redundancy removal mechanism in a complete codec pipeline with entropy coding, bitstream design, and semantic probing evaluation is new. The connection to VCM is well-drawn.

However, the novelty is tempered by several factors: (1) latent-space video compression has antecedents (FVC operates in feature space; Ha & Schmidhuber world models are acknowledged); (2) the semantic probing methodology, while applied here to codecs, is a standard SSL evaluation technique; (3) the GMM entropy model follows Cheng et al. [37] directly; (4) the practical evaluation is restricted to two training clips, limiting the ability to claim the approach "works" rather than "demonstrates feasibility."

**Novelty Score: 6/10.** The combination is original; individual components are largely assembled from existing work. The surveillance-domain focus and VCM framing add positioning value but limit generality. A 7 would require either broader validation or a more fundamental algorithmic contribution.

## 5. Technical Soundness

**Encoder/latent design:** The choice of ViT-Tiny with 16×16 patches producing a 192×16×16 grid is well-motivated. Retaining spatial structure rather than global pooling is correctly justified for both predictor locality and downstream localization (Section 3.2). The affine normalization choice is supported by the ablation.

**GMM entropy model:** Directly following Cheng et al. [37] is sound. The bimodality justification (Section 3.3) — dense background features vs. sparse foreground — is intuitive and consistent with the 5.6× BPP improvement over Laplace. The hyperprior CNN design is standard and credible.

**Predictor architecture:** An 8-layer transformer with 4 context frames is reasonable. The spatial averaging before temporal processing loses spatial context across frames, which is a design trade-off the paper does not discuss. A cross-attention design (attending directly to spatial patches across frames) would be a natural extension that might improve P/I ratios further.

**Training protocol:** The two-phase approach is well-justified and the quantitative evidence for its necessity (Table 8) is convincing. The learning rate differential between components (5×10⁻⁵ vs. 10⁻⁴) is practical but unexplained.

**Surprise metric:** The formula (Eq. 5) — MSE normalized by predicted latent magnitude — is reasonable but non-standard. The ε stabilization term is unspecified numerically. The thresholds τ_high = 0.8, τ_low = 0.4 are set from "observed distributions" without a principled derivation, and as noted in W3, they never activate empirically.

**Reproducibility:** The commitment to releasing code, checkpoints, exact command lines, and random seeds is exemplary. The checkpoint hash practice and Docker container are above standard for the field.

**Red flags:** (1) The non-monotonic RD curve behavior beyond λ=0.05 (Table 4, footnote †) is noted but unexplained. This is a training instability that deserves at least a hypothesis. (2) The BPP computation uses "the entropy model's cross-entropy lower bound" — a standard but optimistic estimate that underreports actual compressed size by 1–5% (acknowledged). This affects the comparison fairness with x265's actual file sizes.

## 6. Experimental Evaluation

**Temporal compression (Section 4.3):** The 61.8% bitrate reduction is measured against the codec's own all-intra mode, not against x265 temporal coding. This is a reasonable within-system comparison but should not be interpreted as a claim against traditional temporal codecs. The slight PSNR improvement (25.13→25.40 dB) is within noise for this scale.

**Semantic probing (Sections 4.4, 4.4.1):** The methodology is the paper's strongest experimental contribution. Training/evaluating on 200/50 frames is very small, and 50 epochs risks overfitting to the limited probe training set. The 86.5% vs. 79.3% class accuracy gap (7.2 pp) at 1.95 BPP is meaningful, but the 94.4% vs. 92.7% gap (1.7 pp) at 0.11 BPP is within the margin of expected variance for 50-frame test sets. A bootstrap confidence interval or paired t-test would be essential for a venue submission.

**Baseline comparison:** Comparing to x265 only is limiting (see W2). The CompressAI comparison in Section 5.5 is ad hoc — no probe evaluation is performed against it, making it impossible to assess whether LeWM-VC's semantic advantage holds against other neural codecs.

**UVG evaluation (Section 5.1, Table 10):** BPP saturating at ~1.95 and PSNR collapsing to 9–17 dB is a striking generalization failure, and the paper deserves credit for including it. The "no-free-lunch" framing is intellectually honest. However, reporting 6 λ values all yielding essentially identical BPP (~1.95) suggests the entropy model has completely failed — the latents are incompressible out-of-domain. A brief analysis of why (distribution mismatch in the GMM hyperprior, encoder features being uninformative) would strengthen the diagnosis.

**LeWM-Eval framework (Section 3.7, Table 3):** The framework is well-conceived and the commitment to automated, verifiable scripts is valuable for the community. The ±5% BPP matching tolerance is reasonable but should be reported as an explicit error bar on semantic comparisons.

## 7. Clarity & Presentation

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized and largely clearly written. The motivation section (Section 1) effectively establishes the machine-consumption thesis. Figures 1–3 are informative — Figure 1 especially provides a clean architectural overview. The ablation table (Table 8) is a model of how ablations should be presented. The limitations section (5.5) is unusually thorough and reads like a senior researcher's honest self-assessment.

**Weaknesses:**

- The abstract makes strong-sounding claims ("62% bitrate reduction," "86.5% class accuracy") without adequate context that these are from a two-clip training regime. The abstract needs a single sentence qualifying the evaluation scope.
- Eq. 1 (IB framing) appears early and prominently but is immediately walked back. This structure creates a misleading impression.
- Figure 4 (surprise distributions) is visually clear but the key finding — that neither video triggers gating — should be stated immediately in the caption rather than buried in the section text.
- Table 1 presents "Temporal bitrate reduction (GOP=8) 61.8%" alongside "x265 (matched)" as a column header, implying a comparison that is not made for this metric.
- The paper conflates BPP computed differently for LeWM-VC (entropy lower bound) and x265 (actual file size) without prominently noting this creates a systematic bias favoring LeWM-VC in rate comparisons.

## 8. Final Recommendation & Score

**Overall Score: 5/10**

**Recommendation: Borderline (lean Reject for top venues; Weak Accept for workshop or specialized track)**

The paper presents a genuine and timely architectural idea — JEPA-based temporal prediction as a replacement for motion vectors in a machine-oriented codec — with admirable honesty about limitations and strong reproducibility practices. However, as currently evaluated, the experimental evidence is too narrow (two training clips, ~100 test frames, no learned codec baselines, a surprise mechanism that never activates) to support the paper's broad framing. The work is better characterized as a well-executed feasibility demonstration than a validated contribution to the state of the art. For NeurIPS or CVPR, this requires substantially expanded evaluation. For a VCM workshop or ICIP, it would be a strong submission.

**Confidence: High** — The technical content is clear, the reproducibility package appears complete, and the experimental design is straightforward to evaluate.

## 9. Suggestions for Improvement (Pre-Venue Roadmap)

### Experimental (Critical)

1. **Expand the dataset substantially.** Train and evaluate on at minimum 10–20 PEViD-HD sequences. Report mean and standard deviation across sequences for all headline numbers. The current two-clip setup cannot support the claim that the codec "works" — only that it can be made to work.

2. **Add learned codec baselines with semantic probing.** Apply the identical probe evaluation protocol to FVC (latent-space predecessor) and at minimum one DCVC variant. This is the key experiment to demonstrate that JEPA-based prediction specifically, rather than neural encoding generally, improves semantic preservation.

3. **Demonstrate the surprise gate actually working.** Either recalibrate thresholds to values where gating activates, or evaluate on a dataset with genuine motion events (e.g., UCF-Crime, ShanghaiTech Anomaly). A mechanism presented as a contribution must be shown to produce its stated effect.

4. **Add statistical significance testing to probe comparisons.** Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals on the class accuracy gap (7.2 pp and 1.7 pp) over the 50-frame test set. At n=50, the 1.7 pp gap may not be statistically significant.

### Technical

5. **Explain the non-monotonic RD curve beyond λ=0.05.** Provide at minimum a hypothesis (quantization noise exceeding the encoder's representational capacity, gradient instability, etc.) and consider showing the training loss curves.

6. **Replace spatial averaging in the predictor with cross-attention.** The current design averages away spatial structure before temporal processing. An ablation comparing spatial averaging vs. full spatial cross-attention across context frames would both improve the method and demonstrate awareness of this trade-off.

7. **Implement actual arithmetic coding on at least one operating point.** Reporting only the cross-entropy lower bound while comparing to x265 actual file sizes introduces a systematic advantage. One end-to-end bitstream measurement would remove this confound.

### Presentation

8. **Rewrite the abstract** to include a one-sentence scope qualifier: "evaluated on a two-clip surveillance dataset as a proof-of-concept demonstration." *(Done in arXiv version.)*

9. **Reorder the IB framing** in Section 1 or move Eq. 1 to the discussion section. In its current position it overpromises the theoretical grounding. *(Partially addressed — softened wording added.)*

10. **Report BPP measurement method explicitly in every table** (lower-bound estimate vs. actual file size) to make the comparison basis unambiguous to readers. *(Partially addressed — note added to metrics section.)*
